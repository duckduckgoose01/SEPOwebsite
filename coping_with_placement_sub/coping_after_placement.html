Coping After Placement Occurs

Adjust to Another ‘New Normal’

Just as parents developed a new ‘normal’ after the child was diagnosed with a disability, parents will have to adjust to a different home life after the child enters residential placement. Life without the child at home will likely bring many competing emotions: sadness, relief, loss/emptiness, peace of mind, worry. Some parents experience mild symptoms of post-traumatic stress. Here is an analogy to explain this seemingly ironic experience: It is not uncommon for our troops at war overseas to carry on with great strength when explosives are going off around them. But sometimes when they return to the calmness of their prior lives at home, they then develop a range of emotional symptoms such as anxiety or depression. This may occur for parents of children with severe challenges too! When they can finally exhale and reflect on all that they have been through after the child is no longer at home, they are free to feel emotions they didn’t have time to feel before. Some families described an eerie sense of calm at home and how they couldn’t figure out what to do with themselves when they weren’t putting out fires all the time. It takes time to get past the fog or confusion before realizing that new things are possible – like seeing a movie or going out to dinner – things they could never do as a family when their child lived at home.

Tend to Other Relationships – and Parents’ Own Self-Care!

When one child has required the majority of parental resources, some parents report feeling guilty that they had neglected their other children, their spouses, extended family members, and important friendships. After their child began residential placement, parents sometimes feel they have more time and energy to devote to these other relationships. Given that research has shown (1) that parenting children with disabilities can be stressful and (2) that stress can negatively impact health, it is important for parents to begin devoting time to their own self-care as well – and to do this without feeling guilty or selfish. This point deserves repeating: parents should take care of their own needs without guilt! Over time, many parents notice that they have time and energy to pursue working again (for those who left their jobs to care for their child), to exercise, or to engage in other hobbies or interests that had taken a back seat since their child was diagnosed.

Adjusting to the New School

Similar to starting a new job where employees have to learn the written and unwritten rules, having a child reside at a new school will involve a similarly complex set of rules to abide by. It takes a while to learn how the school operates and how families are included as part of the team. At this point in a family’s journey, most parents will have developed the ability to ask questions and be assertive (if they didn’t already possess such skills) because of years of navigating many systems while caring for a child with a disability. This will serve parents well as they adjust to the residential school. Even within one school, the rules in each residence (e.g., the adolescents’ house, young adults’ apartments) may be somewhat different. This can be difficult in the beginning when parents are trying to figure out and learn what works best for their child and family, but it will help a great deal to ask questions when uncertain. Parents may find that there is more flexibility in the rules, which may serve more as guidelines than hard-and-fast rules. For example, one facility may state in the parent handbook that family visits may not occur at the residences on weekends. However, if a child exhibits behaviors that make visiting at home or in the community unsafe and parents voice their concerns to the team, supervised visits at the residence may be permitted. It is this kind of dialogue that is ideally encouraged between parents and staff in order for the placement to go well.

Changes in Parents’ Role

One of the most major shifts that parents – particularly the primary caregiver – will undergo is the loss of control of basic parenting functions on a day-to-day basis. This can bring relief when competent teachers are managing the child’s aggression and other serious challenges in a way that no parent could. But there is also the loss of the tender moments and enjoyable aspects of parenting. Whether it is the pleasure parents get from preparing the child’s favorite meal, daily hugs, doing their child’s hair, selecting their clothes, or tucking their child into bed at night – this will all be done by the child’s teachers and staff. When raising children with serious challenges, parents often feel as if they have no choice but to micromanage certain tasks because of their children’s vulnerabilities. Letting go of this level of parental control can be a challenge. One parent could not tolerate seeing how messy his son’s room was, so he arranged for his wife to pick up their son for a weekly dinner at home. The good news is that as parents form relationships with the child’s staff and they become people that both child and parents trust, this becomes easier. Parents know that no one will love their child as much as they do, but they may be surprised at how much the teachers and staff come to love and care for their child too.

Celebrating Small Victories

One mother described her frustration with her daughter’s lack of progress toward walking. Her child had cerebral palsy and autism and was not walking at age 5. The best piece of advice she received from one of her daughter’s doctors was to not compare her progress from one month to the next, but instead from one year to the next. This helped her to adjust her expectations according to her daughter’s disability (rather than to peers who seemed to progress toward walking more quickly). This mother also felt less frustrated with her daughter and felt less dread of physical therapy appointments. She was better able to enjoy her daughter’s progress, in whatever small doses they came in!

Some children take years to develop some form of communication (signs, or via computer program), learn to dress themselves, or toilet independently. One parent was thrilled when she realized that her son could initiate 10 signs one summer but only used 5 the summer before. While this slow pace of progress was sad and disappointing to the parent, she was also able to celebrate the doubling of his vocabulary over the course of one year!

Decrease Guilt!

After a child’s residential placement, families begin to notice the extent of the challenges they faced on a daily basis. Life may feel strangely calmer. Once parents no longer have to manage daily crises and challenges, things can start to feel “normal” again. Families often feel guilty for placing their child outside their home, worrying that others will perceive them as selfish, lazy, or a “bad parent.” It is our belief that obtaining residential care for a child who truly needs it is a gift to the child. The intensity of services means that the child is getting their best chance at achieving their fullest potential. Putting the child’s therapeutic and educational needs before the parents’ own wish to keep the child at home at all costs (even if others in the home – siblings, parents – are being harmed) is a highly unselfish act. Relationships between the child with a disability and their siblings often improve dramatically after residential placement.

Many families feel guilty for enjoying activities, vacations, or holidays without their child. Some parents have found it helpful to remind themselves that their child might not enjoy an airplane ride, or a noisy, crowded party at a relative’s house, but is happier staying at school. These are highly personal and difficult decisions for families to make. Ideally, these decisions should be based on the individual needs and situation of the child and family, rather than driven by feelings of guilt.
